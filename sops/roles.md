# Seasons RP – EMS Unit Certification Guidelines
*Last Updated: 26/04/2026 by S. Bimbo*

## 1. Unit Information

### Synopsis

**Search & Rescue (SAR)**
A Search & Rescue (SAR) EMS Unit is a specialized emergency response team designed to locate, access, stabilize, and extract patients across diverse and high-risk environments, including urban, mountain, wilderness, and water-based incidents. These units bridge the gap between traditional EMS and technical rescue operations by delivering advanced medical care in situations where access is delayed, limited, or dangerous. SAR EMS personnel are cross-trained in advanced life support (ALS) and technical rescue disciplines, allowing them to operate effectively in collapsed structures, high-angle terrain, remote backcountry, and aquatic environments while responding to incidents ranging from natural disasters to missing persons searches. The unit deploys using specialized vehicles, all-terrain equipment, and aviation assets, performing patient assessment, stabilization, and coordinated extrication in challenging conditions alongside fire, law enforcement, and other agencies.

**Aviation (HALO)**
Aviation EMS units are specialized emergency response teams that utilize aircraft to rapidly access, stabilize, and transport patients in time-critical or hard-to-reach situations. These units enhance traditional EMS by providing advanced life support (ALS) in the air, significantly reducing response and transport times in urban, rural, and remote environments. Aviation personnel are highly trained in both critical care medicine and flight operations, enabling them to safely operate in complex conditions such as trauma scenes, search and rescue missions, and mass casualty incidents. Equipped with advanced medical equipment and capable of coordinated deployment with ground and rescue teams, aviation EMS units deliver rapid intervention and efficient patient transport to improve overall outcomes. 

**High Speed Response Specialists (HSRS)**
High Speed Response Specialists are highly trained EMS personnel dedicated to rapid deployment and immediate on-scene intervention in time-critical emergencies. These specialists enhance traditional EMS systems by utilizing agile, fast-response vehicles to navigate traffic, crowded areas, and hard-to-access locations, ensuring faster patient contact. Trained in advanced life support (ALS) and critical decision-making, they provide early assessment, stabilization, and life-saving care in the crucial moments before transport units arrive. Operating as part of an integrated response system, they improve overall efficiency, reduce response times, and support better patient outcomes through rapid, targeted intervention.

**Tactical Command (MASS)**
Tactical Command/Mass Casualty Units are specialized EMS teams designed to coordinate and manage large-scale emergencies involving multiple patients and complex operational demands. These units enhance emergency response by providing structured command, triage oversight, and resource allocation using established incident command systems while working closely with law enforcement to ensure scene safety and unified operations. Personnel are trained in mass casualty management, advanced triage protocols, and interagency coordination, enabling them to operate effectively during disasters, large public events, and high-risk incidents. Equipped with mobile command centers and scalable medical resources, they ensure organized scene control, efficient patient prioritization, and seamless communication between EMS and police to improve outcomes in high-impact situations.

**Residential Doctors (DOC)**
Residential Doctors are medical professionals assigned to provide on-site clinical care and oversight within residential or community-based settings, supporting both routine and urgent healthcare needs. These physicians enhance continuity of care by delivering timely medical assessment, treatment, and coordination with EMS and hospital systems when higher levels of care are required. Trained in primary care and acute intervention, they are equipped to manage chronic conditions, respond to medical emergencies, and guide patient care decisions in real time.

## 2. Search & Rescue (SAR)

### Purpose
The Search and Rescue (SAR) EMS unit is dedicated to locating, accessing, and providing advanced medical care to patients in remote, hazardous, or hard-to-reach environments where traditional response is limited. Their mission is to stabilize and safely extract patients while working in coordination with rescue personnel and law enforcement to ensure rapid, effective, and safe operations. 

### Chain of Command
1. Probationary SAR
2. SAR Paramedic
3. SAR Sr Paramedic
4. SAR Shift Lead
5. **Department Director:** Atlas Buckshank

### Dress Code
* **Wilderness:** High Visibility Coat, EMS Pants, Thick Gloves, Black Boots, Medical Backpack, High Visibility Helmet, Goggles
* **Urban:** High Visibility Coat, EMS Pants, Thick Gloves, Black Boots

### Certification Process
* **Training:** To join Search and Rescue, candidates must first complete three off-road training courses at the Ron Alternative Energy Farm, the Davis Quarry, and Mt. Chiliad. After passing this assessment, they begin training with the HALO unit, including a helicopter safety demonstration. Trainees must then complete a ride-along and successfully perform at least three vertical exfiltrations from a hovering HALO unit over difficult terrain.
* **Certification:** Certification can be received among passing the training courses, as approved by the Department Director. *(Additional certifications like HALO and Scuba available)*

### Procedures

**Urban Rescue:** 1. Locate the victim and approach on foot if accessible; if not, use the HALO unit to rappel and assess the landing surface—land if structurally sound or perform a hovering extraction if not.
2. For confined or underground rescues, operate with a clear extraction plan and a minimum of two SAR personnel.
3. Test air quality with a 5-stage gas analyzer; use full-face positive pressure masks if unsafe for breathing and respirators when air pressure is unstable.

**Mountain Rescue:** 1. Use airborne units in extreme terrain; deploy off-road vehicles like the Cara Cara when ground access is safe.
2. Ensure at least two EMS personnel are active during mountain rescue operations for safety.
3. Coordinate with the HALO unit for extractions—move patients to a landing zone when possible, or conduct an aerial extraction if no landing zone or egress is available.

**Wilderness Rescue:** 1. Determine the last known location, identify natural barriers, and look for tracks to estimate the likely path of travel.
2. Divide the area into quadrants and prioritize searching the most likely areas first.
3. Conduct searches in a buddy system (minimum of two personnel) and remain alert to environmental hazards, including dangerous wildlife.

**Water Rescue:**
1. Water rescues are possible in depths of 10 ft or less; deeper waters require SCUBA gear, as free diving is not sufficient.
2. A minimum of two EMS personnel must be present for all water rescues to ensure safety and prevent further loss of life.
3. Utilize boats and helicopters to locate missing persons and coordinate with the HALO unit when possible.

## 3. Aviation (HALO)

### Purpose
The aviation unit exists to provide rapid, advanced medical response and transport for critically ill or injured patients in areas that are difficult to access by ground. Utilizing helicopters, the unit reduces response and transport times, improving patient outcomes in time-sensitive emergencies. Highly trained flight crews deliver advanced life support care while operating in complex and high-risk environments. The unit also supports search and rescue operations and disaster response.

### Chain of Command
1. HALO Certified
2. SAR HALO
3. **Department Director:** XXX

### General Reminders
* Helicopters are not for joy-rides.
* Helicopters should be landed at Pillbox if not actively pursuing patients.
* Helicopters are meant to be flown in teams of 2.

### Flight Protocols
* **Before Takeoff:** Check weather conditions (avoid flying during rain, fog, or storms). Announce `Air-1` to radio.
* **During Flight:** Stay in legal airspace. Monitor radio.
* **Landing:** Land safely. Turn off rotors before assessing or transporting patients.
* **After Action:** Report any instances via incident reports.

### Certification Process
* **Training:** Complete training sessions with a Department Director or other approved personnel. This covers how to hover, takeoff & landing, controls, emergencies, etc. 
* **Final Test:** Applicants must start at Pillbox, then fly between Tongva Valley and Great Chaparral to Mt Chiliad, then to Paleto Bay, around the Bay and through Raton Canyon and past the Alamo Sea to Sandy Shores, and finally back around the Eastern mountain range to Pillbox.

![Aviation Exam Route](sops/aviationExam.webp)

### Procedures
* **Urban Flight:** Urban flight is rare but if necessary, maintain 1000+ feet of airspace.
* **Outside City Limits:** This is where HALO should mainly be responding. Maintain 500+ feet in airspace.

## 4. High Speed Response Specialists (HSRS)

### Purpose
Designed to deliver rapid medical intervention in time-critical emergencies where every second directly impacts patient survival. The unit utilizes high-performance vehicles and advanced navigation to bypass delays and reach patients faster than traditional ambulance deployment. Personnel are trained to provide immediate stabilization, initiate advanced life support, and coordinate incoming resources for seamless patient care. This team enhances overall system efficiency by bridging the gap between incident onset and full medical transport, improving outcomes in both urban and remote environments. 

### Chain of Command
1. HSRS Certified
2. HSRS Team Lead
3. **Department Director:** David Goggins

### Certification Process
* **Training:** Take a couple laps around the city to gauge your maneuverability of high speed vehicles.
* **Final Test:** We will once again go around the city a couple of times to gauge response time and that you can drive at high speeds safely. Finally, starting at lower Pillbox and go all the way through Paleto Bay and back to Pillbox. This will need to be completed in the STX in 6 minutes and 30 seconds and with minimal damage to the vehicle. *(Additional certification available for motorcycle division with the same testing standards).* ---

## 5. Tactical Command (MASS)

### Purpose
Designed to rapidly respond to incidents involving multiple patients, providing organized triage, immediate life-saving care, and efficient resource management under high-pressure conditions. The unit prioritizes patients based on severity, ensuring that limited medical resources are used to achieve the greatest overall survival. It operates in close coordination with EMS personnel and law enforcement agencies to establish scene control, treatment zones, and transport logistics. Its primary purpose is to stabilize patients quickly and support a structured transition to definitive medical care. 

### Chain of Command
1. MASS Certified
2. MASS Shift Lead
3. **Department Director:** Jackson Hill

### Certification Process
* **Training:** Understand the responsibilities of the MASS unit and pass verbal tests on processes. 
* **Final Test:** Pass a mock mass casualty incident where you have to go about prioritizing care and imitate how to handle MASS situations. 

### Procedures
* **Working with LSPD:** [View LSPD Joint Documentation](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1q1GC2ptNdsPRx6XWYKoOLsFDiJjmnW8BTV24c-Bn7OY/edit?usp=sharing)
* **Arriving to Scenes:** 1. Make sure scene is cleared by PD.
  2. If not, wait for a green light to start assessing and treating patients.
* **Patient Treatment:** 1. Prioritize care by: EMS > PD > Civilians > Suspects.
  2. Clear suspects with PD before resuscitation.
  3. Organize triage back to Pillbox to avoid local traffic.
